Winter Concrete Care: What to Use (and Avoid) on Your Driveway

Not all ice melt products are safe on concrete. Some cause serious surface damage. Here's what to use, what to avoid, and how to protect your investment each winter.

Why Ice Melt Matters for Concrete

Illinois homeowners go through significant quantities of ice melt products every winter, and the product choices directly impact the longevity of concrete driveways and walkways. Using the wrong de-icer — or the right one at the wrong time — can cause surface scaling, spalling, and accelerated deterioration that would otherwise take decades to develop.

The Worst Offenders

Rock Salt (Sodium Chloride)

Rock salt is the most damaging common de-icer for concrete and the most widely sold. It works by lowering the freezing point of water, but in doing so it increases the number of freeze-thaw cycles the concrete experiences within a single storm event. The chloride ions also penetrate the concrete and accelerate corrosion of reinforcing steel. Avoid rock salt on any concrete surface you care about.

Ammonium Nitrate and Ammonium Sulfate

These compounds, sometimes found in fertilizer-based de-icers, chemically attack concrete. They're the worst option for concrete surfaces and should never be used on driveways, walkways, or any concrete surface.

Safe alternatives: Magnesium chloride is generally accepted as the safest widely available de-icer for concrete in moderate applications. Calcium magnesium acetate (CMA) is the most concrete-friendly option but is significantly more expensive. Sand provides traction without chemical damage.

Special Considerations for New Concrete

New concrete — less than one full year old — is especially vulnerable to de-icer damage. The concrete is still in its curing phase and has not yet reached full density and strength. We strongly recommend no chemical de-icers of any kind on new concrete during its first winter. Use sand exclusively for traction until the concrete has had at least one full year to cure and harden.

Plowing and Snow Removal

Metal snowplow blades can chip and scratch concrete, particularly at control joints and edges. Consider asking your plow contractor to raise the blade slightly or use a rubber-edged blade. Hand shovels with plastic blades are preferable to metal for concrete surfaces.

Spring Cleanup

After winter, pressure washing your driveway and walkways to remove residual de-icer, sand, and road salt significantly reduces cumulative surface damage. A thorough rinse in early spring is one of the simplest and most impactful things you can do to extend the life of your concrete.
