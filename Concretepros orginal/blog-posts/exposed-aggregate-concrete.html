Exposed Aggregate Concrete: The Low-Maintenance, High-Texture Option

Exposed aggregate is one of the best-kept secrets in residential concrete. Great traction, natural look, virtually no sealing required. Here's how it's done.

What Is Exposed Aggregate Concrete?

Exposed aggregate concrete is created by removing the surface layer of cement paste from freshly poured concrete to reveal the decorative stone aggregate within the mix. The result is a textured, naturally variegated surface that looks similar to a gravel or stone path but has the durability and structural integrity of concrete.

How It's Made

The process involves pouring standard concrete with a chosen aggregate mix (the stone type and size significantly affect the final appearance), then applying a surface retarder — a chemical that slows the cure of the top layer — before the concrete sets. After the concrete has cured to the right stage, the surface is pressure washed to remove the top layer of paste and expose the aggregate below. Timing is critical: too early and the aggregate pulls out; too late and the paste has hardened beyond what water pressure can remove.

Why It Works Well in Illinois

Exposed aggregate has several practical advantages for McHenry County homeowners. The textured surface provides excellent traction, which is valuable during icy conditions. The varied, natural appearance hides minor surface blemishes and the normal weathering effects better than a smooth finish. Unlike stamped concrete, exposed aggregate does not require sealing to maintain its appearance — though sealing is still beneficial for protecting against de-icer damage.

Best application: Exposed aggregate is particularly well-suited for driveways, sidewalks, pool surrounds, and any surface where traction and low maintenance are priorities. It's also an excellent choice for homeowners who want a more natural, non-uniform aesthetic rather than the geometric precision of stamped patterns.

Aggregate Options

The aggregate mix dramatically affects the final look. Standard mixes use local river gravel in neutral gray, tan, and brown tones. Premium mixes incorporate colored stones, quartz, granite chips, or even glass aggregate for a more distinctive appearance. The stone size also matters — smaller aggregate creates a finer, more uniform texture while larger stone creates a bolder, more dramatic surface.

Cost Comparison

Exposed aggregate concrete typically runs $10–$16 per square foot installed in McHenry County, depending on aggregate type and project size. This positions it above standard broom-finish concrete but below most stamped work — making it an excellent value option for homeowners who want a decorative finish without the full cost of stamping.
