Do You Need a Permit for Concrete Work in McHenry County?

Crystal Lake, Woodstock, Algonquin, and Cary all have different permit requirements. Here's what you need to know — and why we handle the permits for you.

The Short Answer

In most McHenry County municipalities, a permit is required for new concrete driveway installations, significant driveway expansions, and any concrete work within the public right-of-way (including city sidewalk sections). Patios in backyards generally do not require a permit in most cities, but there are exceptions.

City-by-City Overview

Crystal Lake

Crystal Lake requires a permit for new driveway installations and replacements that change the footprint of the existing driveway. Like-for-like replacements (same size, same location) typically do not require a permit, but it's worth confirming with the city's building department. City sidewalk work always requires a permit and must meet city specifications.

Woodstock

The City of Woodstock requires permits for all new impervious surface additions over a certain square footage threshold, including concrete driveways and patios. The city is particularly attentive to stormwater management, so any project that significantly increases impervious surface may require a stormwater review.

Cary

The Village of Cary requires permits for new driveway construction and modifications. Concrete patio work may require a permit if it's within a certain distance of the property line or if it exceeds a size threshold — check with the village before starting.

Algonquin

The Village of Algonquin's permit requirements depend on the scope of work. New driveways and significant expansions require permits. Contact the village building department to confirm requirements for your specific project before proceeding.

We handle permits for you. As part of our standard process on permitted projects, we pull the necessary permits, coordinate inspections, and ensure all work meets local code requirements. This is included in our quoted price — no surprises.

Why Permits Matter

Skipping required permits creates real risks. Unpermitted work can complicate home sales — buyers' attorneys and inspectors often flag unpermitted improvements. In some cases, municipalities require unpermitted work to be removed and redone. Insurance claims related to unpermitted structures can also be denied.

What Happens During Inspection

For permitted concrete projects, an inspector typically visits twice: once to verify base preparation before the pour, and once after completion. These inspections protect the homeowner as much as they ensure code compliance — the base inspection in particular confirms that the work is being done correctly before it's covered up by concrete.
