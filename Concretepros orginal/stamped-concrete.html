Stamped concrete patios start the same as standard patios — proper base prep, forming, and pour — but with the addition of color (either integral color mixed into the concrete or color hardener broadcast on the surface) and decorative stamps pressed into the surface while the concrete is in the correct plastic state. The timing is critical: too early and the stamp sinks in too deep; too late and the concrete is too stiff to receive the pattern cleanly. After stamping, the surface is detailed, saw-cut joints are added where needed, and the patio is sealed with a high-quality concrete sealer to bring out the color depth and protect against staining.

FAQ — Stamped Concrete Patios

Stamped concrete patios should be resealed every 3–5 years to maintain color vibrancy and protect against staining, de-icing salt damage, and wear. Avoid harsh chemical cleaners. Use a low-salt or sand-based ice melt product rather than rock salt during winter. We recommend applying sealer the first fall after installation.
Smooth stamped concrete can be slippery when wet. We address this by adding a light broom texture to the surface during stamping, or by adding anti-slip additive to the sealer. This gives grip while preserving the decorative appearance. Always ask your contractor about slip resistance.
Stamped concrete is a single monolithic slab, which means no individual pieces to shift, settle, or allow weeds between. It's also typically less expensive than quality pavers. The tradeoff is that repairs to a damaged section require more skill to match the color and pattern seamlessly. With proper sealing and care, stamped concrete lasts 25–40 years.
