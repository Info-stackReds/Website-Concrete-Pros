How to Seal Your Concrete Driveway — And When You Actually Need To

Sealing concrete is one of the most misunderstood maintenance topics. Here's the truth about when to seal, what products work in Illinois, and what you can skip.

Do You Need to Seal Your Concrete Driveway?

For standard broom-finish concrete driveways — the most common type in McHenry County — the answer is: it depends on condition and what you're protecting against. Sealing is not mandatory, but it provides real benefits in the right circumstances.

What Sealing Actually Does

Concrete sealer penetrates the surface and fills the pores of the concrete, reducing water and chemical infiltration. In Illinois, the primary benefit is reducing the damage caused by road salt and de-icers, which break down the concrete surface over time. A good sealer also makes the driveway easier to clean and slightly enhances the color of the concrete.

When to Seal

Stamped concrete: every 2–3 years

Stamped and colored concrete should be sealed regularly to protect the color and surface texture. The glossy or satin appearance of a sealed stamped surface is also part of the aesthetic. Use a high-quality solvent-based acrylic sealer designed for decorative concrete.

Standard gray concrete: every 3–5 years

For a standard broom-finish driveway, sealing every 3–5 years is a good practice, particularly if you live on or near a road that gets salted heavily. New concrete should be allowed to cure for at least 30 days before sealing.

When NOT to seal

Don't seal concrete that has active cracks, moisture trapped beneath the surface, or serious spalling — you'll seal in the problem. Address those issues first.

Product recommendation: For Illinois driveways, look for a penetrating silane-siloxane sealer for standard concrete and a solvent-based acrylic for stamped. Avoid water-based sealers on driveways in high-traffic areas.

What De-Icers to Avoid

Rock salt (sodium chloride) is the most damaging common de-icer for concrete. It lowers the freezing point of water and accelerates the freeze-thaw damage cycle. Calcium chloride is less damaging but still not ideal. The safest options are sand for traction and magnesium chloride or calcium magnesium acetate (CMA) de-icers, particularly on newer or decorative concrete.

DIY vs. Professional Sealing

Standard gray concrete sealing is a DIY-friendly project for a homeowner comfortable with a pump sprayer or roller. Stamped concrete sealing is better left to professionals who can ensure even application without lap marks or pooling, which can permanently alter the appearance of the finish.
